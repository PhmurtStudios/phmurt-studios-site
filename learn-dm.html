What Does a DM Actually Do?

3 min read
What You'll Learn
  • The three core responsibilities of a Dungeon Master
  • Why DMing is more fun than it sounds
  • The most common fear new DMs have (and why it's wrong)

The Dungeon Master is the narrator, referee, and world-builder of a D&D game. You describe what the players see, voice the characters they meet, and decide what happens when they try something unexpected. It sounds like a lot, but it boils down to three things.

Your Three Jobs
🎭
Narrator
You describe the world. "The tavern is warm and crowded. A bard strums in the corner." You paint the scene and let players react.
⚖️
Referee
When a player tries something, you decide the DC and interpret the rules. You don't need to memorize everything — just know where to look.
🌍
World Builder
You decide what exists in the world — towns, villains, treasure. This can be as simple as running a published adventure or as deep as creating your own setting.
Your First Decision
Interactive Scenario
Your players walk into a village and the fighter says: "I want to arm-wrestle the blacksmith." This isn't in any rulebook. What do you do?
Not ideal. Saying "no" shuts down player creativity. D&D works best when players feel like they can try anything. Save "no" for things that are truly impossible or harmful to the game.
Great call! You picked a reasonable ability check and set a fair DC. This is the core DM skill — translating player ideas into game mechanics on the fly.
Also great! Sometimes the best response is just to roll with it and make it fun. Not everything needs a dice roll — sometimes roleplay is enough.
Key Takeaway
The number one job of a DM is to say "yes" (or "yes, but...") and keep the game moving. You don't need to know every rule. You need to keep things fun and fair.

Session Prep That Actually Works

5 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How much prep is actually needed (less than you think)
  • The "three things" prep method
  • What to prepare vs. what to improvise

New DMs often over-prepare, writing pages of notes the players will never see. The secret? You only need to stay one step ahead of your players. Most of what makes a session great happens in the moment, not on your prep sheet.

The Three-Thing Method

Before every session, prepare exactly three things. That's it. This keeps you focused and gives you enough structure to improvise around.

Session Prep Simulator — Click each step
1
A strong opening scene. How does the session start? Drop players into action or intrigue immediately. "You wake to the sound of screaming outside your inn" is better than "you're still in the tavern from last time."
2
One or two encounters. A combat encounter, a puzzle, a social challenge — whatever fits your story. Know the monster stats and have a rough map ready. You don't need more than two prepared because sessions rarely go faster than that.
3
A handful of NPCs. Prepare 2-3 named NPCs with a one-line personality and a motivation. "Mara, suspicious merchant, wants to leave town before the festival." That's enough — you'll fill in the rest at the table.
Do Prepare
  • NPC names and one-line personalities
  • Monster stat blocks you'll actually use
  • A rough outline of what could happen
  • A list of random names for improvised NPCs
Don't Prepare
  • Exact dialogue scripts (players won't follow them)
  • Every room in a 50-room dungeon
  • A fixed plot the players must follow
  • Solutions to puzzles (let players surprise you)
Key Takeaway
30 minutes of focused prep beats 3 hours of unfocused writing. Prepare situations, not scripts. Your players will do things you never imagined — and that's exactly the point.

Improv & Thinking on Your Feet

4 min read
What You'll Learn
  • The "Yes, and..." principle adapted for D&D
  • How to handle players going off-script
  • Three improv tricks every DM should know

Your players will go off the rails. That's not a problem — it's the best part. The DMs who thrive are the ones who learn to roll with surprises instead of fighting them. You don't need to be a performer. You just need a few reliable tricks.

The DM's Improv Toolkit
🔄
"Yes, And..."
Accept what players do and build on it. "I climb the chandelier!" — "Yes, and as you swing across the room, the chain starts to give way. Roll Acrobatics."
🎯
"Yes, But..."
Let it work with a complication. "I try to bribe the guard." — "He pockets the gold, but says he has to report it to his captain first. You have ten minutes."
⏸️
Buy Time
When stuck, ask a player to describe what their character does. While they talk, you plan your next move. "How does your character feel about this?"
When Players Surprise You
Interactive Scenario
You've prepared a dungeon. Your players decide to skip it entirely and sail to a different continent. Your notes are useless. What now?
Tempting but risky. Players notice when they're being railroaded. Use this sparingly. If you do redirect, make the reason feel natural and give them meaningful choices within it.
The DM's secret weapon: re-skinning. Your dungeon map becomes a sea cave. Your goblin chief becomes a pirate captain. The treasure is the same. Players never know, and your prep isn't wasted.
We've all been tempted. But a better move is to take a short break, then improvise. Even a rocky session where you wing it teaches you more than ten sessions of scripted play.
Key Takeaway
Prepare situations and NPCs, not plots. When players zig instead of zag, move your prepared content to wherever they go. The story belongs to the table, not your notes.

Building Encounters That Sing

5 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How Challenge Rating (CR) works in practice
  • The difference between easy, medium, hard, and deadly encounters
  • Why interesting terrain matters more than monster stats

Combat is where D&D's mechanical depth shines. A well-designed encounter isn't just a fair fight — it's a puzzle with swords. The key is balancing difficulty so players feel challenged but not hopeless.

Encounter Difficulty Quick Reference
DifficultyWhat It Feels LikeWhen to Use
EasyPlayers win without spending many resourcesRandom encounters, attrition between big fights
MediumA real challenge, but players should winStandard dungeon rooms, most sessions
HardCould go badly — someone might drop to 0 HPBoss room, climactic moments, 1-2 per session
DeadlyGenuinely dangerous — characters could dieFinal boss, desperate last stands, rarely
The Secret: Make the Battlefield Interesting

Monsters standing in an empty room is boring regardless of CR. Add terrain that forces decisions. Consider these elements for every combat:

🪨
Cover & Obstacles
Pillars, overturned tables, rubble. Gives ranged characters and monsters places to hide. Forces movement.
⚠️
Hazards
Lava pools, crumbling floor, magical darkness. Adds risk to positioning and rewards clever play.
🎯
Objectives
Give combat a goal beyond "kill everything." Protect the ritual, stop the alarm, escape before the ceiling collapses.
Key Takeaway
A CR-appropriate fight in an interesting location with a clear objective is more memorable than a perfectly balanced fight in a blank room. Design the battlefield first, then add monsters.

Managing Your Players

4 min read
What You'll Learn
  • How to keep quiet players engaged
  • How to handle rules disputes gracefully
  • Setting expectations with a Session Zero

The hardest part of DMing isn't the rules or the monsters — it's the people. Every table has different personalities, and the DM's job is to make sure everyone has a good time. That starts before the first session even begins.

Session Zero: Set Expectations Early

Before your campaign starts, have a Session Zero where you discuss ground rules, tone, and boundaries. This prevents 90% of table problems.

Common Table Challenges
Interactive Scenario
One player dominates every conversation and talks over others. A quieter player hasn't spoken in 20 minutes. What do you do?
Avoid this. Calling someone out in front of the group creates tension. Handle behavioral issues privately whenever possible.
Perfect in-game solution. Directly inviting a player into the scene by using their character name gives them a natural opening without putting anyone on the spot.
Also excellent. Combining this with option B is the best approach. Address the in-game moment now, then have a kind private conversation later.
Key Takeaway
Be the host of the table, not just the storyteller. Check in with quiet players, redirect spotlight hogs gently, and remember that everyone — including you — is there to have fun.

Running Your First Session

4 min read
What You'll Learn
  • A concrete checklist for your first session
  • The best starter adventures for new DMs
  • What to do when you don't know a rule

You've read the sections above. You know the basics. Now it's time to actually run a game. Here's the truth: your first session won't be perfect, and that's completely fine. Every great DM started with a messy first game.

First Session Prep Checklist
When You Don't Know a Rule
Do This
  • Make a quick ruling and keep moving
  • Write it down to look up later
  • Say "Let's use this for now and I'll check after"
  • Ask the player what their character sheet says
Not This
  • Stop the game for 15 minutes to look it up
  • Argue with a player about rules mid-combat
  • Pretend you know when you don't
  • Let one player become the "rules lawyer" for you
Recommended Starter Adventures
📖
Lost Mine of Phandelver
The gold standard starter adventure. Clear structure, great NPCs, runs levels 1-5. Included in the D&D Starter Set.
🐉
Dragon of Icespire Peak
A more open-ended alternative. Players pick quests from a job board. Great if your group likes freedom. In the Essentials Kit.
✍️
Write Your Own One-Shot
A simple quest: go to a place, fight a thing, get a reward. One dungeon, one boss, 3 hours. The best way to learn by doing.
Key Takeaway
The best DM advice in two words: just start. Your first session will be imperfect and that's what makes it yours. Every mistake is a lesson, and your players will remember the fun, not the fumbles.
